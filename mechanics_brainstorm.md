# Mechanic Brainstorm
The key to making this land is that the ship fuel rewards feel immediately good while the player fuel rewards feel useful — so the player's instinct is always to sacrifice the useful for the good. 

## HIGH PLAYER FUEL
Concrete mechanical tools. Valuable, but you have to stop and appreciate them.

Double Jump — standard platformer reward, dramatically opens up traversal
Dash — short-range blink dodge, makes combat feel fluid and responsive
Suit Grip — lets you cling to walls and ledges briefly, reach new areas
Scanner Pulse — reveals hidden parasites, structural weak points, and secret passages through walls
Overclock — briefly slows time during combat, expensive to use
Carry Capacity — hold more items/fuel canisters simultaneously
Efficient Burn — your passive fuel drain rate is halved, making everything else last longer
Grapple Tether — pull yourself to anchor points across gaps
Melee Strike — a close-range bash that staggers enemies and costs no fuel
Hazard Filter — lets you pass through toxic/hot/irradiated zones without damage
Terminal Access — hack ship consoles to unlock shortcuts, lore, or bypass puzzles
Structural Override — manually force open jammed blast doors
Thermal Sight — see heat signatures through walls, track enemies before engaging
Sprint — run speed dramatically increased, trivialises many platforming challenges
Stability Field — immune to knockback and physics effects (useful in low-gravity areas)
Deep Lung — survive without oxygen for much longer in compromised corridors
Weapon Augment — your weapon gains a secondary charged fire mode
Proximity Alert — your suit pings you before enemies notice you, window to avoid combat
Auto-Stabiliser — fall damage is negated; you land perfectly from any height
Resonance Map — your suit builds a complete map of any area you've visited, with notes


## HIGH SHIP FUEL
Immediately pleasurable. Sensory rewards. The dopamine hits.

The Lights Come On — proper warm lighting floods the ship section; it looks beautiful for the first time
The Soundtrack Plays — ambient music resumes, gentle and spacey; the silence is over
The Crew Relaxes — NPCs stop cowering, sit together, start talking warmly to each other
The Observation Deck Unseals — a panoramic window to the stars opens; the universe stretches out
The Ship's Cat Reappears — a small orange cat emerges from hiding and begins following you around
The Coffee Machine Works — you can make a cup. Your character sits for 8 seconds and sighs contentedly
Colour Returns — the desaturated palette of the area blooms back into full warm colour
The Jukebox Activates — a recreation room jukebox plays; crew members drift toward it
The Greenhouse Wakes Up — plants in the biodome section visibly perk up and flower; it smells good (the narrator says so)
The Ship's AI Tells You a Joke — a genuinely good joke. The crew laughs
The Star Map Illuminates — a beautiful full holographic star chart spins in the navigation room
Crew Gifts — a crewmember leaves something at your bunk: a drawing, a note, a piece of food
The Shower Works — you can take a hot shower. Your character makes a small happy sound
Windows Unshutter — previously-sealed portholes open to show the galaxy drifting past
The Galley Opens — a proper meal is cooked; a short cutscene plays of the crew eating together
Warm Announcements — the intercom starts making gentle, reassuring ship announcements ("Good morning. Oxygen is nominal.")
Save Stations Glow — save points look welcoming and warm instead of functional and clinical
The Hologram Lounge — crew members use recreational holograms, play games; you can join
Archive Unlocks — a library of ship history, art, and crew personal logs becomes available, told warmly
The Ship Hums — the engines produce a low, resonant, reassuring tone. It feels like the ship is alive and well


## LOW PLAYER FUEL
Challenging, manageable, but grinding. The game becomes work.

Jump Height Reduced — you can't reach platforms that were previously trivial
Suit Noise — your degrading suit clicks and hisses, alerting enemies in adjacent rooms
Weapon Drift — your aim slowly wanders during sustained fire; precision becomes effortful
Interaction Lag — pressing a button or terminal takes 2-3 seconds of struggling animation
Tunnel Vision — peripheral vision fades; awareness of the environment narrows
Stumble — occasionally your character trips mid-corridor on flat ground
Fuel Gauge Inaccuracy — your own fuel readout flickers and misreports, hard to manage
Grip Failure — you can no longer hang from ledges; drops become unavoidable
Fall Vulnerability — fall damage is now significant; you have to path carefully down
Carry Drop — when hit, you have a chance to fumble and drop held items
Slower Healing — med kits take twice as long to apply while your hands shake
Vision Static — HUD elements flicker in and out; minimap becomes unreliable
Enemy Aggro Range Expands — your laboured breathing and suit sounds carry further
Combat Cooldowns Double — special abilities take longer to recover between uses
Door Struggle — heavy blast doors require a minigame to open rather than a button press
NPC Dialogue Slurs — you can't follow conversations properly; miss information or hints
No Sprint — you cannot run. The ship feels enormous and slow
Weak Strikes — melee attacks no longer stagger; enemies shrug them off
Oxygen Sensitivity — you start taking damage in areas that were previously borderline-safe
Intrusive Readouts — your suit keeps interrupting with low-fuel warnings; the alerts obscure gameplay


## LOW SHIP FUEL
Immediately aversive. The player feels it in their gut.

Alarms Don't Stop — a continuous klaxon plays. Not urgent enough to be exciting, exactly persistent enough to be maddening
Lighting Strobes Red — rooms cycle between blackout and red emergency light; genuinely disorienting
The Ship Groans — structural stress sounds play randomly, loud enough to make you flinch
Doors Slam Unexpectedly — airlocks and corridor doors snap shut mid-traversal, requiring rerouting
The Map Corrupts — navigation data degrades; rooms you've visited lose their layout markers
Crew Members Panic — NPCs shout, cry, argue; the audio landscape becomes distressing
The Music Becomes Wrong — if any track was playing, it degrades into distorted, unpleasant noise
Gravity Destabilises — random fluctuations launch you slightly off the ground or make you heavy; movement becomes unpredictable
The AI Becomes Frantic — the ship's computer delivers rapid-fire status warnings in an increasingly desperate voice
Temperature Warnings — the screen edges frost or shimmer with heat; a constant reminder something is wrong
Save Stations Offline — the game explicitly warns you it cannot save here; stakes feel higher
Static Floods the Screen — periodic full-screen static bursts, brief but jarring, interrupting your sense of flow
The Narrator Escalates — the narrator's tone shifts from helpful to pressuring, repeating the same anxious instructions, second-guessing you
Colour Drains — the world desaturates area by area until it's nearly monochrome; it looks bleak
The Cat Hides — small environmental storytelling: the cat disappears. The ship feels more hostile
Crew Becomes Unhelpful — NPCs stop offering dialogue options; they're too frightened to engage
Enemy Spawn Rate Increases — the ship's degraded state seems to embolden parasites; more appear
Observation Deck Seals — the beautiful view is locked away again; that comfort is gone
The Intercom Blares Static — not quite signal, not quite silence; an uncomfortable in-between that plays on loop
The Ship Breathes Wrong — the background ventilation hum, which you stopped noticing when it was healthy, returns as an irregular, laboured sound. The ship sounds like it's suffering.


A design note worth sitting with: items 1 and 20 from each list form a beautiful pairing — the most functional player unlock (Double Jump) is purely useful, while the most powerful ship unlock (The Ship Hums) is purely felt. That gap is where the whole game lives.
